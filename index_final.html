Benjamin Banneker

first African-American astronomer

Having been born to two free parents, Benjamin escaped a life of slavery and pursued a life of science, exploration and invention. Some of his most notable works include developing an irrigation system to control water flow to the crops from nearby springs to his farm, as well as building America's first clock that strikes on the hour.

Arthur B.C. Walker Jr

pioneer of extreme ultraviolet optics

Arthur Bertram Cuthbert Walker Jr. was a solar physicist and a professor at Stanford University from 1974 until his death in 2001, where he mentored 13 graduate students, majority of whom came from underrepresented groups. His first graduate student, Sally Ride, went on to become the first American woman in space.

Mercedes Richards

astrophysicist

Mercedes Richards was president of IAU Commission 42: Close Binary Stars, a member of the Board of Advisers of the Caribbean Institute of Astronomy and a councilor of the American Astronomical Society. Her research focused on computational astrophysics, stellar astrophysics, exoplanets and interacting binary stars systems.

Guion Bluford, Jr.

first African American in space

Guion Bluford, Jr. was chosen to become a NASA astronaut in August 1979 out of thousands of possible candidates. He participated in four Space Shuttle flights between 1983 and 1992. In 1983, as a member of the crew of the Orbiter Challenger on the mission STS-8, he became the first African American in space.

Mae Jamison

first woman of color in space

Mae Jamison went into orbit aboard the Space Shuttle Endeavour on September 12, 1992. She resigned from NASA in 1993 and founded the Jemison Group, a company that researches, markets, and develops science and technology for daily life. She has also founded the Dorothy Jemison Foundation for Excellence and is the current principal of the 100 Year Starship organization.

Neil deGrasse Tyson

astrophysicist, author

Since 1996, Dr. Neil deGrasse Tyson has been the Frederick P. Rose Director of the Hayden Planetarium. He has hosted scientific TV and radio shows, including StarTalk and Cosmos: A Spacetime Odyssey, and has written numerous books on astronomy. The U.S. National Academy of Sciences awarded Tyson the Public Welfare Medal in 2015 for his "extraordinary role in exciting the public about the wonders of science".
